# Introduction

Social engineering is a cyberattack technique in which attackers manipulate people into revealing confidential information or performing actions that compromise security. Unlike traditional attacks that target software vulnerabilities, social engineering exploits human psychology, including trust, curiosity, fear, and urgency.

It is considered one of the most effective attack vectors because people are often easier to manipulate than computer systems are to hack. According to Verizon's *2025 Data Breach Investigations Report (DBIR)*, a significant proportion of data breaches continue to involve the human element, including phishing, stolen credentials, and social engineering.

# 1. Phishing Attacks :

## Definition

Phishing is a cyberattack in which attackers impersonate legitimate organizations or individuals to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, banking details, or credit card numbers.

## Types of Phishing

### 1. Spear Phishing

A targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization using personalized information.

### 2. Whaling

A phishing attack specifically targeting high-level executives such as CEOs, CFOs, or directors.

### 3. Vishing

Voice phishing where attackers use phone calls to impersonate banks, government agencies, or technical support.

### 4. Smishing

SMS phishing where malicious text messages contain fake links or urgent requests.

## How Phishing Works -

1. The attacker creates a fake email, message, or website.
2. The victim receives the message.
3. The victim clicks a malicious link or downloads an attachment.
4. Credentials or sensitive information are stolen.
5. The attacker uses the stolen information for fraud or unauthorized access.

## Real-World Case Study

### Google and Facebook Business Email Compromise (2013–2015)

A Lithuanian attacker, Evaldas Rimasauskas, impersonated a legitimate hardware supplier and sent fraudulent invoices to Google and Facebook. Employees approved payments, resulting in losses of over **$100 million** before the fraud was discovered.

## Prevention Recommendations

- Verify email sender addresses carefully.
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
- Never click suspicious links or attachments.
- Conduct regular employee phishing awareness training.

# 2. Pretexting

## Definition

Pretexting is a social engineering attack where an attacker creates a believable false identity or story (pretext) to convince victims to disclose confidential information.
## How an Attacker Builds a False Scenario

1. Collects information about the victim.
2. Creates a convincing identity.
3. Gains the victim's trust.
4. Requests confidential information or system access.

---

## Real-World Case Study

### Twitter Insider Attack (2020)

Attackers contacted Twitter employees while pretending to be internal IT support staff. Using social engineering techniques, they convinced employees to reveal credentials, allowing attackers to access internal systems and compromise several high-profile Twitter accounts.

---

## Prevention Measures

- Verify identities before sharing sensitive information.
- Implement strict identity verification procedures.
- Provide regular employee awareness training.

---

# 3. Baiting

## Definition

Baiting is a social engineering attack that offers something attractive to victims in exchange for compromising their security.

---

## Types of Baiting

### Physical Baiting

Attackers leave infected USB drives in public places, hoping someone plugs them into a computer.

### Digital Baiting

Attackers distribute fake software downloads, pirated content, or free applications that contain malware.

---

## Real-World Case Study

### Infected USB Drive Experiments

Security researchers have demonstrated that USB drives intentionally left in public areas are often plugged into workplace computers by curious employees, potentially allowing malware to execute automatically.

---

## Prevention Measures

- Never connect unknown USB devices.
- Download software only from trusted sources.
- Disable automatic execution of removable media where appropriate.

---

# 4. Quid Pro Quo (Bonus)

## Definition

Quid Pro Quo attacks involve attackers offering a service or benefit in exchange for sensitive information.

Example:

An attacker pretends to be IT support and offers to "fix" a computer problem if the employee provides their username and password.

---

## Prevention

- Verify IT support requests through official channels.
- Never share passwords.
- Train employees to recognize social engineering tactics.

---

# Organisational Recommendations

## Employee Security Awareness Training Checklist

- Conduct regular phishing simulation exercises.
- Train employees to verify suspicious emails and phone calls.
- Encourage immediate reporting of suspicious activities.
- Require Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for sensitive systems.
- Keep employees informed about the latest social engineering techniques.

---

# Conclusion :

Social engineering attacks continue to be among the most effective cyber threats because they exploit human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities. Phishing, pretexting, baiting, and quid pro quo attacks can lead to credential theft, financial loss, and data breaches. Organizations can significantly reduce these risks by combining employee awareness training, strong authentication methods, clear verification procedures, and regular security assessments.
